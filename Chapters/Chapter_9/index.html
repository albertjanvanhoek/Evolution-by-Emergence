    Common Threads in Evolving Systems

    As proposed by the paradigm and supporting research , many evolving systems, whether living or not, share common characteristics. They arise from components with vast combinatorial possibilities (Principle 8). They are subject to processes generating diverse configurations within a dynamic network (Principle 2). Crucially, selection mechanisms favor configurations exhibiting functions like stability or persistence (Principle 5, abstractly applied). Key traits include:

    • Combinatorial Richness: Systems are composed of diverse components (e.g., elements) that can combine in vast numbers of ways.

    • Configuration Generation: Processes (e.g., geological changes) exist that explore and produce numerous different configurations (e.g., potential mineral formulas).

    • Selection for Function: Mechanisms preferentially select configurations based on advantageous functions (e.g., thermodynamic stability leading to persistence).

    This interplay between variation generation and selection, driven by feedback within the network (Principle 3), can drive systems toward increased complexity or patterning over time, irrespective of whether they are alive.
